TL;DR — modular addition (Lin §3–4), reproduced

What & why. Learn \(a+b \bmod p\) (\(p=29\)) from the full grid of \(N=p^2=841\) input pairs (train on a fraction \(\alpha\), test on the rest). We reproduce Lin's two claims: (i) the top eigendirections of the per-class eNTK track the Fourier features the network actually learns, and (ii) the eNTK spectrum diagnoses grokking (delayed generalization). Three models: the quadratic MLP (Lin/Gromov), a ReLU MLP (our variation), and her 1-layer transformer.

Result 1 — all three grok. Train accuracy saturates early; test accuracy jumps to 100% much later (curves below). Caveat: the transformer only grokked once we matched Lin's exact train/test split (Python random.shuffle, seed 2) — it is split-sensitive, whereas the MLPs grok robustly across splits.

Result 2 — cliffs emerge with grokking. The per-class eNTK spectrum is flat at initialization and develops sharp "cliffs" (contiguous eigendirection blocks bounded by sharp spectral drops) as the model groks. The cliff span is the learned-feature subspace (emergence panel below shows the sharpening over training).

Result 3 — dense (MLP) vs sparse (transformer) features. Both solve the task in a Fourier basis, forming \(\cos(\omega_k(a+b))\) from the \(a,b\) components via the product-to-sum identity $$\cos(\omega_k a)\cos(\omega_k b)-\sin(\omega_k a)\sin(\omega_k b)=\cos\!\big(\omega_k(a+b)\big), \qquad \omega_k=\tfrac{2\pi k}{p}.$$ The eNTK cliff dimension counts the features used, \(\;\dim(\text{cliff})=\underbrace{2}_{\cos,\sin}\times\underbrace{2}_{a,b}\times(\#\text{frequencies})\):

  • Quadratic & ReLU MLPs use all \(\lfloor p/2\rfloor=14\) frequencies → dense 56-mode cliff (edges at 57/113 after a leading DC/mean mode). ReLU shows the same cliffs — the structure is not unique to the quadratic activation (a new positive result).
  • Transformer commits to a few seed-dependent key frequencies (this run, seed 2: \(k=1,12\)) → a small ~8-mode cliff (edges ~5/9). Same task, far sparser features, and a correspondingly lower-rank eNTK. The number/identity of key frequencies are not universal — they vary seed to seed.

Takeaway. The eNTK cliff is a direct readout of feature economy: the same problem solved densely (MLP, ~56 modes) or sparsely (transformer, ~8 modes). Pick an experiment below for its grokking curve, cliff spectrum, and cliff-emergence-over-training, plus the per-model math.

What is the grokking "solution"? (click to expand)

Two phases. Early in training the network memorizes the training pairs — train accuracy hits 100% while test accuracy sits near chance (\(1/p\)). It has fit the data with a lookup-like circuit that does not generalize.

The generalizing circuit (the "clock" / Fourier-multiplication algorithm). Driven by weight decay, the network slowly reorganizes into an algorithm that actually computes \(a+b \bmod p\): it places each input on a circle at a few frequencies, \(a \mapsto (\cos\omega_k a,\ \sin\omega_k a)\) with \(\omega_k = 2\pi k/p\); the nonlinearity multiplies the \(a\)- and \(b\)-components to form $$\cos(\omega_k a)\cos(\omega_k b)-\sin(\omega_k a)\sin(\omega_k b)=\cos\!\big(\omega_k(a+b)\big);$$ and the unembedding reads off the answer as the \(c\) that maximizes \(\cos\!\big(\omega_k(a+b-c)\big)\). This circuit generalizes to unseen pairs.

Grokking = the delayed handover. Test accuracy jumps when the generalizing circuit overtakes memorization. Weight decay drives it: the Fourier solution has much lower weight norm than the memorization solution, so regularization pressure favors it — but it forms slowly, hence the long delay. (With weight decay removed, grokking is absent or indefinitely delayed.)

Why the eNTK sees it. The cliff is the generalizing circuit's feature set — the Fourier directions appear as the eNTK's top eigenspace. At init and during pure memorization there is no cliff; it sharpens exactly as test accuracy climbs (the emergence panel). The number of cliff modes equals the number of frequencies the circuit uses — dense for the MLP (all 14), sparse for the transformer (a few key frequencies).

Refs: Nanda et al., Progress measures for grokking via mechanistic interpretability (2023); Gromov, Grokking modular arithmetic (2023, the analytic quadratic-MLP solution).
